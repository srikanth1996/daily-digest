The conflict entered its most dangerous phase yet. The United States and Israel have struck Iran's Natanz nuclear enrichment facility in a coordinated attack, while Iran simultaneously launched missiles near Israel's nuclear research centre at Dimona. Air raid sirens rang out across Jerusalem overnight as the conflict went nuclear — in the political if not yet the physical sense.

On the military front, Israel struck targets in both Tehran and Beirut in a widening campaign. The US has dispatched Marines to West Asia for the first time since the conflict began, signalling a potential ground component. A British nuclear-powered submarine has been confirmed in the Arabian Sea, adding a third Western military force to the theatre. Iran responded with drone strikes on US-aligned regional bases.

On the diplomatic front, President Trump is openly threatening attacks on Iranian power plants, while simultaneously reversing his earlier permissive stance on Hormuz. Trump's shifting course has raised questions in Washington and Delhi about US strategic coherence. Japan's Foreign Minister Motegi floated Hormuz minesweeping operations if a ceasefire is reached — an unprecedented offer from Tokyo.

For India, the pain intensifies. Crude oil remains above one hundred and ten dollars per barrel. Sri Lanka has already raised fuel prices by twenty-five percent, a warning sign for South Asian fuel markets. Indian refineries are urgently sourcing alternate crude from West Africa and Russia. The Indian diaspora in the Gulf faces evacuation contingencies, with MEA monitoring over two hundred thousand nationals in the conflict zone.

Watch tomorrow for any Iranian strike on US naval assets, and whether India activates strategic petroleum reserves.
